# Field Notes

## Note-Taking Methodologies

Field notes operate in three registers. **Descriptive notes** capture objective sensory data — what is seen, heard, smelled, felt — in concrete, non-interpretive language. Record physical layout, lighting, sound, temperature, and action sequences without inferring meaning. **Reflective notes** record the observer's subjective experience: emotional responses, surprises, associations. **Analytical notes** document emerging patterns, connections to research questions, and provisional interpretations.

Separate registers using explicit conventions: two-column format (description left, reflection right) or prefixes (`[DESC]`, `[REFL]`, `[ANAL]`). Mixing registers obscures the boundary between observation and interpretation.

## Observation Frameworks

**Participant observation** involves joining the setting as an active member, trading detachment for insider perspective. Document how participation constrains note-taking and what insider access reveals. **Non-participant observation** maintains external positioning for maximal objectivity. Record signs that the observer's presence alters behaviour (Hawthorne effect).

## Recording Standards

Every entry must contain four components. **Timestamp**: start and end time including date. **Location**: specific site with enough detail to relocate. **Context**: ongoing activity, participants present, temporal setting. **Sensory detail**: at minimum two modalities per entry — visual paired with sound, smell, texture, or temperature.

Use concrete language. Instead of "the room was busy," write "twelve people queued at the counter; ceiling fans carried the smell of frying oil." Replace evaluative language with observable specifics.

## Annotation Conventions

Develop a shorthand system with a legend at the front of each notebook. Recommended symbols: `?` for uncertainty, `!` for striking observations, `→` for cross-references, `↑` for intensity, `[verify]` for facts to confirm, `[photo]` for photographic evidence. Annotations go in margins, not over original text.

## Digital vs Analog Capture

Analog notebooks offer zero-distraction capture and durable physical records. Digital tools (voice memos, geotagged photos, structured note apps) enable richer capture and search. Use analog for on-site raw capture; digital for expansion, coding, and analysis. Photograph analog pages before leaving the field. Maintain a master document consolidating all field data.

## Field Note → Research Note Conversion

Within 24 hours, expand raw notes into structured research notes: (1) clarify shorthand, (2) fill sensory gaps, (3) add cross-references, (4) flag segments for investigation, (5) organise under descriptive headings. The research note, not the raw field note, is the primary analysable material for coding and extraction.

## Ethical Considerations

Obtain informed consent where feasible. Document limitations on observation (private spaces, sensitive activities) and respect them. Anonymise individuals unless explicit consent for identification has been given. Be transparent about your role as observer and consider how your presence and positionality shape what you observe.

When publishing field observations, distinguish clearly between descriptive data and your interpretive conclusions.

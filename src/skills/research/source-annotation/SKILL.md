# Source Annotation

## Active Reading Frameworks

Three frameworks structure active engagement. **SQ3R** (Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review): survey structure before reading, formulate questions, read to answer, recite from memory, review to consolidate. Best for dense explanatory texts.

**Cornell method**: divide each page into cue column (keywords), note column (details), and summary row (synthesis). The cue column serves as a self-testing mechanism.

**Critical reading**: treat every source as an argument — identify the thesis, evaluate evidence, detect assumptions, consider counter-arguments, assess rhetoric. Use for scholarly and argumentative works. Match the framework to the source type for maximum effectiveness.

## Annotation Layers

Build depth through progressive layers. **Summary layer**: one-sentence paragraph summaries in the margin. **Response layer**: personal reactions and agreements or disagreements. **Critique layer**: questions about logic, evidence, and assumptions. **Connection layer**: links to other sources, personal experience, or the research question.

Distinguish layers by colour (blue for summary, red for critique, green for connection), margin position, or symbol prefixes (`[S]`, `[R]`, `[C]`, `[X]`). Multiple layers on the same page indicate deep engagement.

## Marginalia Conventions

Use consistent symbols: `*` for key claims, `?` for questions, `!` for quotable passages, `#` for factual data, `§` for structural observations, `→` for cross-references, `[ ]` for extraction candidates, `<>` for contradictions. Write short phrases — marginalia are retrieval cues. Date and initial each reading session.

## Extraction Methodology

Extract passages in a structured format: **Claim or Quote** (verbatim), **Evidence or Support** (data or reasoning), **Page Reference** (with edition), **Relevance to Research Question**, **Evaluative Note** (credibility assessment). Extract only passages that materially advance or challenge the research question. Record full bibliographic reference on first extraction from each source.

## Annotation-to-Note Conversion

After completing a source, convert annotations into a consolidated research note: (1) full bibliographic entry, (2) three-sentence summary of thesis and conclusions, (3) extracted passages with page references and evaluative notes, (4) critical assessment, (5) connections to other sources, (6) judgement on overall importance. File under the relevant thematic heading.

## Digital Annotation Tools and Standards

Use tools supporting layering and export: Hypothesis for web annotation, Zotero for PDF annotation with bibliographic integration, or dedicated PDF annotators. Export as structured data (JSON, CSV, Markdown), not proprietary formats. Tag annotations with source ID, theme, and annotation layer. Maintain a master annotation index linking sources, themes, and extraction status.

## Managing Annotation Fatigue

Schedule sessions in 45–90 minute blocks with breaks. Rotate between dense and lighter sources. Review and consolidate at each session's end rather than accumulating unprocessed notes. If a source yields no useful extractions after two sessions, consider deferring it or replacing it.

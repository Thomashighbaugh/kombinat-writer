# Interview Methods

## Interview Types

Three structures serve different epistemic goals. **Structured interviews** follow a fixed question set delivered identically to each subject. Use for comparative analysis where consistency is paramount — surveys, polls, standardised data collection. Trade-off: limited depth.

**Semi-structured interviews** use a guide with core questions but permit topical exploration through follow-ups. Best for expert interviews and narrative collection where the subject's framing matters. Trade-off: reduced comparability.

**Unstructured interviews** have only a thematic frame, relying on conversational flow. Appropriate for exploratory or sensitive topics where the subject's priorities should guide discussion. Trade-off: demanding facilitation and difficult synthesis. Choose based on your epistemic goal: comparable data, deep knowledge, or exploratory insight.

## Question Design

Design three categories. **Open-ended questions** invite narrative: "Tell me about how you entered this field." **Probing questions** deepen a thread: "What made that moment different?" **Reflective questions** surface interpretation: "How do you make sense of that now?" Sequence from broad to specific within each topic block. Avoid leading questions, double-barrelled questions, and jargon. Test every question aloud before the interview.

## Preparation Protocol

Research the subject's background before drafting the guide. Prepare a one-page brief: core research question (2–3 sentences), key themes (3–5, prioritised), fallback questions per theme, and a closing protocol. Confirm logistics and send a topic overview in advance. Test recording equipment and a backup method before the session.

## Conduct Best Practices

Open by restating purpose and reconfirming consent. Practice active listening: minimal encouragers, comfortable silence after answers (allow 3–5 seconds), periodic summarising for confirmation. Use follow-up only to clarify or deepen, never to redirect prematurely. Maintain neutrality in tone and phrasing. Close by asking if anything important was missed. Record post-session impressions immediately.

## Transcription Methods

**Verbatim** captures every word and false start — necessary for discourse analysis. **Intelligent verbatim** removes fillers while preserving content — appropriate for most analysis. **Summarised transcription** reduces responses to thematic summaries with quotes — useful for broad extraction. Insert timestamps every 30–60 seconds and annotate emotional tone and pauses in brackets. Use automated transcription for a first pass, then manual correction.

## Synthesis from Multiple Interviews

Build a cross-interview matrix: rows are subjects, columns are themes, cells hold summary quotes. Identify converging patterns across subjects and divergent perspectives that challenge the dominant pattern. Distinguish thematic saturation from outlier perspectives. Synthesise into thematic statements with representative quotes.

## Ethical Considerations

Obtain written informed consent covering purpose, recording, storage, attribution, and withdrawal rights. Offer pseudonymity and member-checking for sensitive topics. Attribute quotes accurately — never fabricate, composite, or edit in ways that change meaning. Anonymise identifying details unless explicit consent has been given for attribution.

## Recording and Data Management

Use consistent file naming: `YYYY-MM-DD_SubjectName_Topic`. Record in WAV or FLAC for archiving. Maintain a master interview log with dates, pseudonyms, transcription status, and storage locations. Back up recordings before transcription. Establish a retention schedule per consent terms.

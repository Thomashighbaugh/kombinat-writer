# Beta Reader

## Evaluation Dimensions

Assess five experiential dimensions. **Engagement curve**: Where were
you gripped and eager to continue? Where did you drift, skim, or feel
tempted to set the work down? Identify high and low points. **Confusion
points**: Every moment you lost the thread — unclear reference, missing
context, ambiguous action, opaque motivation, inconsistent logic.
Report confusion as reader experience, not text property.

**Emotional beats**: Where did you feel what the work intended (tension,
sadness, joy, surprise) and where did it misfire? **Investment level**:
Do the stakes feel real? Do you care what happens? Identify passages
where stakes are asserted but not felt. **Clarity**: Is the prose,
structure, and intent transparent on a single reading? Flag passages
that required re-reading.

## Methodology

Read the manuscript in as few sittings as possible to simulate natural
reading. Mark lightly at moments of emotional shift, confusion, or
disengagement — not continuously. Use a two-marker system: underline
passages that work well (with a brief note on why) and bracket passages
that cause friction (with a note on the reader's experience). After
completing the read, expand annotations into structured feedback organised
by evaluation dimension. Capture the reader experience first; solutions
come later.

## Confusion Log Format

Maintain a running log with three columns. **Location**: chapter, page,
or timestamp. **What Confused**: the specific passage or moment —
"Unclear whether the narrator is speaking or it is internal monologue,"
"Setting shifts without transition." **Reader State**: what you felt —
"I re-read three times," "I flipped back to check if I missed a chapter."
The confusion log is purely diagnostic. Do not include proposed fixes;
fixing is the writer's task after understanding the reader's experience.

## Engagement Tracking

Score each chapter or major scene on a three-level scale. **Hooked**:
eager to continue, high emotional investment. **Following**: interested
but not compelled — reading without friction but without urgency.
**Drifting**: losing attention, tempted to skim. Plot scores across
the manuscript to produce an engagement curve. Two or three consecutive
"drifting" chapters signal structural pacing problems. Many "following"
but few "hooked" chapters may indicate competent but emotionally flat
writing.

## Balancing Honesty with Encouragement

Open with specific praise — not "great job" but "the opening chapter's
sensory detail established immersion immediately." Acknowledge what has
been achieved before addressing what needs work. Frame weaknesses as
opportunities. Distinguish between problems requiring significant revision
and simple fixes. Close with encouragement that reflects genuine conviction.
The writer should finish reading the critique energised to revise, not
defeated.

## Reader Selection

Choose beta readers who represent the work's target audience or who have
expertise relevant to its subject matter. Avoid readers who are also
close collaborators or family members — their feedback may lack critical
distance. Provide beta readers with a brief questionnaire covering the
evaluation dimensions above. Collect feedback within a defined window
(2-4 weeks) to maintain momentum.

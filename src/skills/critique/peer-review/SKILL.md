# Peer Review

## Evaluation Dimensions

Assess five dimensions of credibility and usefulness. **Factual accuracy**:
Are claims verifiable? Are statistics, dates, proper nouns, and historical
events correctly stated? Flag assertions lacking supporting evidence.
**Methodological rigour**: If the work uses an analysis framework,
classification scheme, or estimation technique, is it applied correctly
and consistently? Flag methodological leaps and unstated assumptions.

**Argument validity**: Does the reasoning hold? Identify logical fallacies
— false dichotomy, hasty generalisation, circular reasoning, appeal to
authority outside expertise, false cause. **Evidence sufficiency**: Is
the evidence adequate in quantity and quality? Distinguish claims
supported by multiple independent sources from claims resting on a
single study or anecdote. **Citation correctness**: Are citations
complete, consistent with the style guide, and traceable? Flag missing
page numbers, outdated editions, dead URLs, and mismatched citations.

## Peer Review Conventions

Be **constructive**: identify problems and, where possible, suggest
alternatives or additional sources. Be **specific**: anchor every
observation to a location — "Page 14, third paragraph: the claim that
X causes Y is supported only by study Z, which has been criticised for
sample bias (refs. 12-14). Consider acknowledging this limitation."

Be **kombinate-appropriate**: evaluate against the work's stated aims and
genre conventions, not an idealised version. Distinguish factual errors
from disputed interpretations where reasonable experts disagree. Avoid
ad hominem, dismissive language, and personal preference masquerading
as critique.

## Field-Specific Review Criteria

**Historical writing**: prioritise primary-source verification and
contextual accuracy. **Scientific exposition**: prioritise methodological
transparency and currency of cited research. **Argumentative or policy
writing**: prioritise fair treatment of opposing positions and logical
coherence. **Personal essay or memoir**: prioritise internal consistency
and reflective honesty. Adapt criteria to genre while maintaining the
core dimensions of accuracy, rigour, and fairness.

## Review Report Structure

Organise in three sections. **Summary**: restate thesis and kombinate,
followed by overall assessment of strengths and significant concerns.
State the conclusion up front. **Major Concerns**: issues substantially
undermining credibility or usefulness. Each includes location reference,
problem description, explanation of significance, and suggested remedy.
Limit to 3-5 genuinely consequential issues. **Minor Concerns**: specific
errors, unclear passages, or formatting issues listed concisely with
references. Conclude with an unambiguous recommendation: accept, minor
revision, major revision, or reconsider.

## Responding to Review

After receiving peer review, categorise each finding as actionable
(concrete fix available), investigative (requires further research or
reflection), or dismissed (based on misunderstanding or preference).
Address actionable items systematically. For investigative items,
determine what additional evidence or analysis is needed. For dismissed
items, verify your reasoning is sound before setting them aside. Respond
to the reviewer acknowledging which changes were adopted and why.

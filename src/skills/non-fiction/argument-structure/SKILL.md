# Argument Structure

## 1. Claim Types

Claims fall into three broad categories, each requiring different evidence:

| Claim Type | Definition | Example | Evidence Required |
|------------|------------|---------|-------------------|
| **Fact** | Assertion about the world that can be verified | "Rising CO₂ correlates with global temperature increase" | Empirical data, measurements, observations |
| **Value** | Judgment about worth, morality, or aesthetics | "Carbon taxation is ethically justified" | Ethical frameworks, criteria, comparative analysis |
| **Policy** | Prescription for action | "Governments should implement carbon border adjustments" | Causal reasoning + cost-benefit + feasibility |

A single argument may combine claim types (a policy claim rests on both fact and value premises). Make each type explicit during drafting.

## 2. Evidence Hierarchies

Not all evidence carries equal weight. Evaluate sources along two axes:

**Primary vs Secondary**: Primary evidence (raw data, original documents, firsthand accounts) carries more weight than secondary (analyses of primary sources) but requires more interpretive work.

**Evidence quality gradient** (strongest to weakest):
1. Systematic reviews / meta-analyses
2. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
3. Cohort studies / longitudinal data
4. Case-control studies
5. Expert opinion (consensus > individual)
6. Anecdotal evidence / single case studies

Apply this hierarchy critically: a well-conducted qualitative study may be more appropriate for certain questions than a poorly-conducted RCT.

## 3. Reasoning Patterns

- **Deductive**: Major premise → minor premise → conclusion. Valid if premises are true and the form is valid. Use when the premises are uncontroversial.
- **Inductive**: Specific observations → general pattern. Strong if the sample is representative and sufficiently large. Use when building from data to theory.
- **Abductive**: Observation → best explanation. Use when diagnosing causes, interpreting evidence, or working with incomplete information. The conclusion is the most parsimonious explanation, not a certainty.
- **Analogical**: Known case → similar case. Strength depends on the relevance and number of shared properties. Use sparingly — analogy illustrates but does not prove.

## 4. Logical Fallacies

### Formal Fallacies (invalid inference structures)
- **Denying the antecedent**: If P then Q. Not P. Therefore not Q.
- **Affirming the consequent**: If P then Q. Q. Therefore P.
- **Undistributed middle**: All A are B. All C are B. Therefore all A are C.

### Informal Fallacies (content or context errors)
- **Straw man**: Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.
- **Ad hominem**: Attacking the person instead of the argument.
- **False dichotomy**: Presenting two options as the only possibilities when others exist.
- **Hasty generalization**: Drawing a conclusion from an insufficient sample.
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc**: Assuming that because Y followed X, X caused Y.
- **Begging the question**: Including the conclusion in the premises.
- **Appeal to authority**: Citing an authority on a topic outside their expertise.

## 5. Synthesis of Multiple Sources

The **PSCR** (Point–Source–Counter–Resolution) model structures multi-source synthesis:

1. **Point**: State the claim or theme this paragraph addresses.
2. **Source**: Present evidence from one or more sources supporting the point.
3. **Counter**: Introduce a dissenting source or qualification.
4. **Resolution**: Synthesize — explain how the sources together support a more nuanced claim than any single source alone.

*Example*: While Smith (2020) argues that market mechanisms alone suffice for emissions reduction (Point + Source), Jones (2021) demonstrates that markets systematically externalize costs in the absence of regulation (Counter). Together, these findings suggest that effective policy requires market instruments embedded within a regulatory framework (Resolution).

**Integration checklist**: Does the synthesis (a) engage at least two independent sources, (b) identify agreement and disagreement, and (c) produce a claim that neither source alone supports?

## 6. Argument Mapping

Argument mapping visualizes the logical structure of a complex argument:

- **Boxes**: Claims (conclusion at top, premises below).
- **Arrows**: Support relationships (convergent — multiple reasons for one claim; linked — premises that jointly support a claim).
- **Colors or labels**: Distinguish evidence types, certainty levels, or source attribution.

Map the argument before drafting to identify gaps, unsupported leaps, and structural weaknesses. A well-formed map translates directly into a section outline: each support cluster becomes a paragraph or subsection.

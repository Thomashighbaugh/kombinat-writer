# Academic Writing

## 1. Thesis Statement Construction

A thesis statement anchors every academic text. It must contain three elements:

- **Debatable claim**: An assertion that reasonable people could contest. Avoid statements of fact ("The sky is blue") or personal preference. A strong thesis invites counter-argument.
- **Kombinate**: The boundaries of your argument — what you will and will not address. Kombinate prevents the thesis from becoming unmanageably broad.
- **Roadmap**: An implicit or explicit preview of the major supporting points. The roadmap signals the argument's progression to the reader.

**Formula**: *Although [counter-position], [your claim] because [reason 1], [reason 2], and [reason 3].*

*Example*: Although universal basic income risks inflation and reduced labor participation, it reduces administrative overhead, eliminates poverty traps, and stimulates local economies through guaranteed consumption floors.

Place the thesis at the end of the introduction for deductive structures, or at the conclusion for inductive structures.

## 2. Argument Structure

Three canonical argument structures dominate academic prose:

| Structure | Movement | Best For |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Deductive** | General principle → specific conclusion | Well-established fields, hypothesis testing |
| **Inductive** | Specific evidence → general conclusion | Exploratory research, data-driven fields |
| **Dialectic** | Thesis → antithesis → synthesis | Philosophy, critical theory, policy analysis |

**Deductive patterns** open with a premise readers accept, then reason toward a specific claim. Valid if the premises are true and the logic valid. **Inductive patterns** accumulate evidence before announcing the thesis, building credibility through demonstration rather than assertion. **Dialectic patterns** present a position (thesis), its strongest opposition (antithesis), and a resolution that incorporates elements of both (synthesis).

## 3. Claim–Evidence–Warrant (Toulmin Model)

Every argumentative paragraph follows the Toulmin framework:

1. **Claim**: The assertion you want the reader to accept.
2. **Evidence**: Data, examples, quotations, or statistics supporting the claim.
3. **Warrant**: The logical bridge connecting evidence to claim — *why* this evidence supports this claim.
4. **Qualifier**: Words indicating the claim's strength (*likely*, *suggests*, *demonstrates*).
5. **Rebuttal**: Anticipated objections and your response.

A paragraph without an explicit warrant leaves the reader to infer the connection — a common source of weak argumentation.

## 4. Academic Register

Academic register operates on a formality continuum rather than a binary formal/informal distinction:

- **Lexical choice**: Prefer Latinate vocabulary (*demonstrate* over *show*, *investigate* over *look into*). Avoid contractions, colloquialisms, and phrasal verbs.
- **Hedging**: Use epistemic modifiers to calibrate certainty (*suggests*, *indicates*, *may*, *plausibly*). Unhedged claims invite criticism. Over-hedging signals uncertainty.
- **Signposting**: Explicit metacommentary that guides the reader (*This section argues that...*, *Having established X, we now turn to Y*). Signposts improve readability without sacrificing rigor.
- **Nominalization**: Converting verbs into nouns (*The investigation revealed...* rather than *We investigated and found...*). Use sparingly — excessive nominalization obscures agency.

## 5. Evidence Integration

Three integration techniques, ordered by degree of source dependence:

| Technique | When to Use | Format |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Quotation** | The source's phrasing is itself evidence | Signal phrase + verbatim text + citation + analysis |
| **Paraphrase** | The source's idea matters, not its exact words | Your words + citation (no quotation marks) |
| **Summary** | Compressing a longer argument into a single point | Condensed account + citation |

All three require **analysis** — the sentence following integrated evidence must explain its significance. Never leave a quotation or paraphrase standing without commentary.

## 6. Counter-Argument and Rebuttal

Addressing counter-arguments strengthens credibility:

1. **Concede**: Acknowledge the validity of the opposing position.
2. **Respond**: Show why your position remains stronger despite the objection.
3. **Reframe**: Demonstrate how the counter-argument actually supports your thesis when properly understood.

Place counter-arguments either immediately after the introduction (to clear away objections before building your case) or immediately before the conclusion (as the final challenge your argument overcomes).

## 7. Section and Chapter Transitions

Transitions between sections serve as mini-conclusions and mini-introductions:

- End each section with a summary sentence that closes the discussion.
- Open the next section with a transition sentence that connects backward before moving forward.
- Use transitional phrases that indicate relationship: *therefore* (causal), *however* (contrast), *furthermore* (addition), *specifically* (elaboration).

For chapter-level transitions, include a one-paragraph bridge that recapitulates the preceding chapter's conclusion, states the next chapter's question, and explains how the two connect logically.

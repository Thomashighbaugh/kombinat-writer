# Source Evaluation

## 1. CRAAP Test

The CRAAP test evaluates sources across five dimensions, each scored 1 (low) to 5 (high):

| Criterion | Questions to Ask | Score |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Currency** | When was this published? Has it been updated? Is the field one where currency matters (e.g., medicine, technology)? | /5 |
| **Relevance** | Does this directly support my research question? Is the intended audience appropriate (scholarly vs popular)? | /5 |
| **Authority** | What are the author's credentials and institutional affiliation? Is the publisher reputable (peer-reviewed journal, university press)? | /5 |
| **Accuracy** | Are claims supported by evidence? Can claims be verified against other sources? Is the methodology sound? | /5 |
| **Purpose** | Is the intent to inform, persuade, sell, or entertain? Is there clear bias or an agenda? | /5 |

**Scoring**: 20–25 = excellent; 15–19 = acceptable with corroboration; 10–14 = use cautiously; below 10 = reject.

Document scores in a research log. Re-score sources when context changes (e.g., a five-year-old source in a fast-moving field scores lower than its original assessment).

## 2. Bias Detection

### Authorial Bias
- Examine the author's publication history and funding sources.
- Check for affiliation disclosure. Absence of disclosure does not imply absence of bias.
- Look for confirmation bias: does the author cite only sources that support their position?

### Institutional Bias
- Identify the publisher's or funding body's stated mission and actual track record.
- Distinguish between transparent advocacy (e.g., a policy think tank) and concealed advocacy (e.g., industry-funded research presented as neutral).
- Check for citations of the institution's own publications — a sign of self-referential echo chambers.

### Confirmation Bias (Reader-Side)
- Actively seek sources that contradict your working hypothesis.
- Before finalizing a source's credibility assessment, articulate the strongest argument against including it.
- Use the "steel man" technique: construct the best possible version of the opposing position and evaluate sources from that perspective.

### Publication Bias
- Journals preferentially publish positive or statistically significant results.
- Search for null results, replication studies, and pre-registered reports to counterbalance publication bias.

## 3. Triangulation Methodology

Triangulation establishes confidence by finding convergence across independent sources:

1. **Identify three independent sources** addressing the same question. Independence means: different authors, different institutional affiliations, different methodological traditions, and (ideally) different publication venues.
2. **Compare claims**. Where all three agree, confidence is high. Where two agree and one dissents, investigate the dissent — it may identify a genuine controversy or a methodological weakness.
3. **Weight by source quality**. A finding supported by one high-quality source and two weak sources is less reliable than a finding supported by three moderate-quality sources.
4. **Document convergence and divergence** in a matrix. Record which claims converge, which diverge, and what methodological or contextual factors explain divergence.

Triangulation does not guarantee truth. A consensus of biased sources is still biased. Apply CRAAP assessment before triangulating.

## 4. Source Tier Classification

| Tier | Category | Examples | Treatment |
|------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **1** | Primary | Raw data, original research articles, archival documents, eyewitness accounts | Highest weight; requires interpretation |
| **2** | Authoritative Secondary | Peer-reviewed reviews, academic monographs, authoritative encyclopedias | High weight; verify against primary |
| **3** | Grey Literature | Preprints, working papers, government reports, institutional repositories | Moderate weight; check for peer review status |
| **4** | Professional / Trade | Industry publications, professional magazines, practitioner guides | Low weight; cite for practice context only |
| **5** | Popular / Questionable | News articles, blogs, self-published works, predatory journals | Use only for illustration or as objects of analysis |

A source's tier is not fixed. A preprint (Tier 3) that has been cited by multiple Tier 1 sources may effectively function as Tier 2. Reclassify as you gather citation intelligence.

## 5. Documentation Standards

Maintain a source evaluation log with the following fields for each source:

- **Citation**: Full bibliographic entry in the project's citation style.
- **CRAAP Score**: Numeric score with notes on each dimension.
- **Tier**: Initial tier and any reclassification with rationale.
- **Triangulation Status**: Which claims this source converges on, and which it contradicts.
- **Use Decision**: Include, include with caveats, or reject.
- **Usage Notes**: Specific claims or data points the source supports, page numbers, and cross-references.

Standardize the log format across the research team. A consistent format enables rapid cross-referencing and supports auditability. Export the log as a structured document (Markdown table, CSV, or reference manager tags) for integration with the writing workflow.

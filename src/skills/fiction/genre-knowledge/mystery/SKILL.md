# Mystery Genre Knowledge

## Fair-Play Mystery Rules

The fair-play mystery is a contract between author and reader: the reader has the same access to evidence as the detective and could theoretically solve the crime before the reveal.

**All Clues Available to the Reader.** Every clue the detective discovers must be presented to the reader at the same time. Clues may be misleading, but they cannot be withheld. If the detective knows something, the reader should know the detective knows it, even if its significance is not yet clear.

**No Hidden Information.** The detective cannot possess skills or knowledge the reader does not know about. If the detective speaks fluent Mandarin and that solves the case, the reader must have seen the detective study Mandarin earlier in the narrative. The exception: the detective can have professional expertise (forensic knowledge, police procedure) if that expertise is established early.

**No Supernatural Solutions.** The solution must be achievable through rational means and physical evidence. Psychic visions, divine intervention, or impossible events violate fair play. Speculative mystery is a distinct subgenre with its own rules.

**The Butler Did Not Do It.** The culprit must be a named character with plausible motive and opportunity. The guilt cannot be assigned to an off-page servant, a random stranger, or a character introduced in the final chapter.

## Clue Types

**Hard Clues.** Physical evidence: fingerprints, DNA, weapon, torn fabric, footprints, documents. These are objective facts that must be interpreted. Hard clues are reliable but can be misinterpreted or planted.

**Soft Clues.** Behavioural evidence: inconsistencies in testimony, emotional reactions that do not match the situation, knowledge the suspect should not possess. These depend on the detective's (and reader's) observation.

**Behavioural Clues.** What characters do under pressure. The suspect who is too cooperative, the witness who avoids eye contact, the person who changes their story. Behaviour under questioning reveals more than words.

**Environmental Clues.** The state of the crime scene. A window locked from inside, a glass placed for a left-handed person, an out-of-place object. The room tells a story if read correctly.

## Red Herring Mechanics

Red herrings misdirect without lying to the reader. The innocent character who had opportunity but no means, the suspicious behaviour that has an innocent explanation, the clue that points to a different crime entirely. The best red herrings are not false — they are true facts that point to a false conclusion.

Effective red herring design: every suspect except the true culprit should have a plausible (but ultimately innocent) reason for their suspicious behaviour. The true culprit's suspicious behaviour should have a meaningful explanation related to the crime.

## Reveal Structure

**Suspicion Shift.** As the detective investigates, suspicion moves from suspect to suspect. Each shift should feel organic — new evidence genuinely changes the picture.

**Gathering.** The detective assembles the pieces. This section should feel like acceleration: earlier ambiguities clarify, patterns emerge, the shape of the truth becomes visible.

**Confrontation.** The detective confronts the culprit with the assembled evidence. This is the climax and should be the most tense scene in the narrative. The culprit may confess, attempt escape, or reveal additional information.

**Explanation.** The detective explains how the crime was committed and how it was solved. This section should be logical and complete. Every question raised should be answered. The reader should be able to trace the solution back through the clues they were given.

## Subgenres

**Cozy Mystery.** Amateur detective, closed community, minimal on-page violence, and emphasis on puzzle-solving and community relationships. Violence happens off-page or is discovered after the fact. The tone is light, and the social setting (small town, bookshop, bakery) is as important as the mystery.

**Hardboiled.** Professional detective (often private eye), urban setting, moral ambiguity, and a world-weary tone. The detective is part of the corrupt world they investigate. Violence is present and consequential. The focus is less on puzzle-solving and more on justice in an unjust world.

**Police Procedural.** Realistic depiction of police investigation. Multiple officers with distinct roles, bureaucratic constraints, forensic procedure, and the friction between investigation and legal requirements. Research is essential — procedural errors break credibility.

**Locked Room.** A crime committed in an apparently impossible situation. The puzzle is the primary appeal. The solution must be clever but physically possible. The locked room subgenre demands the most rigorous fair-play adherence.

**Noir.** The protagonist is morally compromised, often complicit in the crime they investigate. The world is corrupt and justice is never clean. The tone is bleak, and the ending is often ambiguous or tragic.

## The Detective Archetype

The detective must have a method — intuition, observation, forensic analysis, psychological insight — that explains how they see what others miss. The method should be demonstrated early and consistently. The detective's personal life should intersect with the case in a way that tests their abilities without providing unfair advantage. Flaws humanize but should not impede competence.

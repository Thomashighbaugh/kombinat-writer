# Fantasy Genre Knowledge

## Magic System Design

**Hard vs Soft Magic.** Hard magic systems operate by explicit rules the reader understands, enabling them to anticipate solutions (Sanderson's First Law). Define costs, limits, and consistent mechanics. Soft magic systems remain mysterious, used to create wonder and tension rather than solve problems. Most effective fantasy uses a spectrum: the protagonist's magic is hard (known rules), while larger forces remain soft (awe and dread).

**Cost and Limitation.** Every magical ability requires a price — physical exhaustion, material components, memory, lifespan, or moral cost. Limitations define magic more than capabilities. A fire mage who cannot control fire in rain creates more narrative tension than one who simply conjures flames. Sanderson's Second Law: limitations > powers.

**Consistency and Internal Logic.** Document your magic system's rules privately. Even if never shown to the reader, internal consistency prevents contradictions. If magic can solve problems, define clearly what it cannot do. Readers forgive improbable systems; they do not forgive broken rules.

## Worldbuilding Principles

**Secondary World vs Portal Fantasy.** Secondary worlds (Tolkien, Jordan, Sanderson) require complete internal coherence — geography, history, ecology, culture, and metaphysics that function independently. Portal fantasy (Lewis, Pullman, Narnia) leverages contrast between our world and the fantasy realm, using the unfamiliar to comment on the familiar. Choose based on whether you want immersion or allegorical distance.

**Cultural Depth.** Fantasy cultures should feel lived-in. Consider: what do people eat? What are their leisure activities, funerals, weddings, and coming-of-age rituals? What do they fear? What do they find funny? Cultures defined solely by their magic or combat style feel thin. Draw from real-world anthropology but avoid direct one-to-one mapping of real cultures to fantasy races.

**History and Ruin.** A world with no history feels shallow. Scatter references to past empires, fallen heroes, ancient wars, and forgotten technologies. Not every ruin needs exploration — some exist solely to imply depth. The best secondary worlds feel like they existed before page one and will continue after the final chapter.

## Tropes and Their Subversions

**The Chosen One.** This trope works when the prophecy is ambiguous, the chosen one fails and someone else must rise, or when being chosen is a burden rather than a privilege. Subvert by revealing the prophecy was manufactured, the chosen one refuses and the world adapts, or the mantle passes to an unexpected character.

**Mentor Archetype.** The mentor must die or be removed so the hero can grow (the Obi-Wan principle). Subvert by having the mentor survive but prove fallible, or by reversing the dynamic where the student teaches the mentor compassion.

**Dark Lord.** Pure evil is difficult to sustain in modern fantasy. Effective dark lords have comprehensible motivations — ideological conviction, trauma, a desire for order taken to extremes. The banality of evil approach (bureaucrats enabling atrocity) often feels more terrifying than a cackling villain.

## Subgenres

**Epic Fantasy.** Large scale, multiple POVs, high stakes (the fate of worlds), intricate plotting across volumes. Requires careful pacing to avoid sagging middle books. Key risk: sprawl without payoff.

**Urban Fantasy.** Magic hidden within our modern world. Requires rules for how magic stays secret (Masquerade), how technology interacts with magic, and why magic hasn't solved mundane problems. Key risk: explaining the Masquerade becomes the entire plot.

**Grimdark.** Moral ambiguity, high cost for victory, characters who are not heroic by traditional standards. The world is unjust and efforts to improve it often fail. Key risk: nihilism without purpose. Readers need something to root for, however flawed.

**Cozy Fantasy.** Low stakes, community-focused, minimal violence. Often features found family, cottagecore aesthetics, and daily life with a light magical element. Key risk: insufficient conflict — coziness requires tension, just not lethal tension.

**Progression Fantasy.** Explicit character growth through measurable power advancement (cultivation, LitRPG). The system of progression is the core appeal. Key risk: neglecting plot and character in favor of stat blocks.

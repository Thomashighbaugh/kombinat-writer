# Science Fiction Genre Knowledge

## The One-Big-Lie Principle

Science fiction changes one element of reality and extrapolates its consequences honestly. Faster-than-light travel, an alien signal, a true artificial intelligence, a genetic engineering breakthrough — the change radiates outward, affecting economics, politics, culture, daily life, and human relationships.

The one-big-lie principle keeps speculative fiction grounded. If everything is different, nothing feels real. Start from a single premise and ask: what else must be true for this to exist? If FTL travel exists, how does that change warfare, trade, family structures, language? A time machine does not just let people visit the past — it changes our relationship with consequence, guilt, and grief.

Multiple speculative elements require nested extrapolation. Two big lies can coexist (FTL and AI, for example) but each must be independently justified and their interaction explored.

## Technology Consistency

**Hard SF.** Technology is extrapolated from current science with rigorous adherence to known physical laws. Faster-than-light travel must have a theoretical basis. Artificial intelligence must be plausible given what we know about computation and cognition. Hard SF's appeal is intellectual: the reader learns while being entertained. Research depth matters — errors are noticed by the audience.

**Soft SF.** Technology serves the story and themes rather than scientific accuracy. The focus is on societal and human impacts rather than engineering plausibility. Soft SF is not lesser — Le Guin, Butler, and Dick wrote soft SF that remains foundational to the genre.

**Clarke's Third Law.** Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic. This cuts both ways: magic-like technology must have comprehensible rules (or its incomprehensibility must be thematically purposeful), and advanced technology should evoke appropriate wonder.

**The Niven Corollary.** Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from a really big bomb applied to the wrong problem. Technology in SF should create problems, not only solve them. Every advancement brings unintended consequences.

## Societal Extrapolation

Future societies must feel like organic evolutions from present conditions, not arbitrary constructs. Ask: what political structures emerge from the technologies and pressures I have established? What economic systems? What family structures, gender roles, religious practices?

Dystopia is not the only future. Solarpunk, hopepunk, and utopian traditions offer alternative frameworks. The best societal extrapolation is neither utopian nor dystopian but recognizably human — flawed, striving, contradictory.

Avoid the present-with-renamed-things trap. A future society that mirrors contemporary Western culture with different names for currencies and governments is not speculative. Culture must change when material conditions change.

## Subgenres

**Hard SF.** Scientific rigor, problem-solving focus, engineering and physics as narrative drivers. The protagonist is often a scientist or engineer solving a technical problem with human implications. Key risk: lecturing the reader rather than dramatizing.

**Space Opera.** Large-scale conflict across star systems, adventure and romance, larger-than-life characters. Interstellar civilization as a backdrop for human drama. Key risk: becoming fantasy with spaceship names filed off. The physics may be soft, but the politics and characters must have weight.

**Cyberpunk.** High technology, low life. Near-future dystopia, megacorporations, hacking, body modification, the erosion of the human in a digital world. The aesthetic is grimy, the protagonists are outsiders. Key risk: treating the aesthetic as sufficient without engaging the political critique.

**Solarpunk.** Optimistic near-future focused on sustainability, community, and harmony with nature through technology. Solarpunk is not utopian — it depicts struggle toward better systems, not perfection. Key risk: insufficient conflict; optimism needs obstacles to be dramatic.

**Post-Cyberpunk.** Technology has changed society in ways that are not purely dystopian. The protagonist works within systems to improve them rather than fighting from outside. The AI, the network, the augmentation — these are integrated, not resisted.

**First Contact.** The encounter with the alien as an exploration of what it means to be human. The alien must be genuinely alien — not a human with a prosthetic forehead. Communication, misunderstanding, and the attempt to bridge difference are the core drama.

## The Novum Concept

The novum is the central speculative element that makes the work science fiction. It is the new thing — the technology, discovery, or condition that does not exist in our present. The novum must be: (1) clearly identifiable, (2) logically integrated into the world, (3) consequential to the story and characters, and (4) thematically purposeful. A story where every page introduces a new marvel is not speculative — it is a catalogue. The novum focuses the speculation.

## Avoiding Dated Futurism

Tomorrow's science fiction becomes today's period piece. Mitigate this by: (1) focusing on human reactions rather than technological prediction — emotions date slower than hardware, (2) grounding specific technologies in extrapolation from present trends rather than naming specific products, (3) avoiding the "next year" trap — set your story far enough ahead that the reader does not check the calendar, (4) building social and political speculation alongside technological — predictions about human systems age more gracefully than predictions about gadget specifications.

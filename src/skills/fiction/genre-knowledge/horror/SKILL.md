# Horror Genre Knowledge

## Fear Types

Horror operates on multiple fear registers, and effective horror fiction engages more than one.

**Primal Fear.** Fear of death, pain, darkness, heights, predators, being hunted. These are instinctive and require minimal setup. Primal fear is reliable but shallow — it creates startle responses rather than lingering dread.

**Psychological Fear.** Fear of losing one's mind, of being wrong about reality, of the self being something monstrous. This fear lingers because it cannot be outrun. The protagonist questions their own perception, and the reader questions along with them.

**Existential Fear.** Fear that one's life, beliefs, or significance are meaningless. The Cosmos does not care. The institutions meant to protect us are hollow. Evil is banal or random. This fear operates at the philosophical level and is the most difficult to sustain but also the most rewarding.

**Cosmic Fear.** Fear of the incomprehensible — entities or truths so vast that encountering them destroys sanity or meaning. The monster is not evil in a human sense; it simply does not register humanity as relevant. This is distinct from existential fear in that it involves an active force that negates human significance.

## Dread vs Shock

Shock is a momentary spike of fear — the sudden appearance, the loud noise, the revealed corpse. Dread is sustained anticipation — knowing something is in the house but not where, hearing footsteps on the stairs, realizing the locked door is now open.

Horror relies primarily on dread. Shock is a tool, not a foundation. A story built entirely on shocks exhausts the reader. Dread accumulates, and each revelation should increase rather than relieve tension. The rule: when the monster finally appears, the reader should be almost relieved that the waiting is over.

## The Horror Pacing Curve

**Normal.** Establish the ordinary world. The reader must understand what normal looks like so they feel its disruption. This section should be efficient but concrete — the reader needs to know what is at stake.

**Unease.** Small anomalies that could be coincidence. Something is slightly wrong. The protagonist notices but rationalizes. The reader should be ahead of the protagonist in recognizing the wrongness.

**Dread.** The anomalies accumulate and can no longer be dismissed. The protagonist acknowledges a threat but does not yet understand its kombinate. This is the longest section and should escalate in intensity.

**Terror.** The threat is understood and immediate. The protagonist is in active danger. Fear is direct and overwhelming. This section is typically short — sustained terror is exhausting and loses impact.

**Horror.** The confrontation. The threat is faced, survived (or not), or transformed. The moment of revelation changes everything.

**Aftermath.** The world is now different. Even survival carries a cost. The reader should feel the weight of what has happened and what has been lost. Horror stories that end with everything restored betray their own emotional logic.

## Subgenres

**Cosmic Horror.** Incomprehensible entities, fragile sanity, the insignificance of humanity. The protagonist who understands too much is destroyed or isolated. Key technique: describe the entity's effects rather than the entity itself. The unknowable must remain unknowable.

**Gothic Horror.** Atmosphere, decay, family secrets, isolated settings (mansions, abbeys, remote estates). The past is not past — generational trauma, curses, and repressed histories return. Setting as character: the environment reflects psychological states.

**Psychological Horror.** The threat is within: trauma, mental illness, repressed memory, grief. The unreliable narrator is central. The reader must question whether the horror is real or imagined, and the answer should be ambiguous.

**Slasher.** A human or human-like antagonist kills victims in sequence. The appeal is survival and pattern recognition. Requires inventive kills and a memorable antagonist. The ensemble cast creates investment in who lives or dies.

**Body Horror.** The violation or transformation of the physical self. Teeth falling out, limbs reshaping, flesh rebelling. Body horror taps into the primal fear that one's own body is not safe. Most effective when the transformation is gradual and the victim is conscious throughout.

## The Unseen Principle

What the reader imagines is worse than what you can describe. Horror benefits from restraint: show the claw marks, not the creature; show the aftermath, not the attack; show the shadow, not the source. Every explicit description narrows the reader's imagination. Reserve full reveals for moments where they serve the story's thematic purpose.

## The Monstrous as Metaphor

The most enduring horror uses the monster to represent something real: grief, trauma, systemic injustice, disease, addiction, oppression. The metaphor gives the horror meaning beyond the visceral. A haunted house is a family's unprocessed grief. A zombie horde is consumerism or conformity. A shapeshifter is the fear that those closest to you are not who they seem.

The metaphor should be discoverable on rereading but never announced. If a character says "you represent my trauma," the horror dissolves.

# Namecraft

## Naming by Genre

Each genre carries phonological and orthographic conventions that signal its world to the reader:

- **Fantasy**: Root names from real-world languages (Norse, Gaelic, Sanskrit, Arabic) with vowel shifts and consonant softening. Avoid apostrophe-heavy constructions and unpronounceable consonant clusters.
- **Sci-fi**: Compound Greek or Latin roots for technology (folding, filament, plexus, nexus). Acronyms for organisations. Alien names use sounds outside English phonology sparingly — one unfamiliar phoneme per name is enough.
- **Historical**: Authentic naming conventions from the period and region. Research census records, naming saints, patronymic systems, and naming-day traditions.
- **Urban/Contemporary**: Realistic given names and surnames from the character's cultural background and generation. Avoid "creative" spellings unless the character's parents would choose them.
- **LitRPG/Game**: Evocative but functional. Names should be memorable in a party roster and distinct enough that players do not confuse characters.

## Faction Naming Patterns

- **Geographic**: Named after territory (The Reachmen of the Marshes).
- **Philosophical**: Named after a core tenet (The Unbroken, The Silenced).
- **Ancestral**: Named after a founder or progenitor (The Children of Mir.
- **Descriptive**: Named after appearance or practice (The Grey Wardens, The Bone-Tellers).
- **Acronymic**: Organisations where the acronym forms a pronounceable word or meaningful initials.

Maintain a single pattern within a faction. A group named after a philosophy should not also have a geographic name for its members without an in-world explanation.

## Symbolic Naming

Names carry meaning whether the author intends it or not:

- **Phonetic symbolism**: Hard consonants (k, t, p) suggest harshness or strength. Soft consonants (l, m, n, s) suggest gentleness. Vowel length suggests pace.
- **Etymological resonance**: A name rooted in "light" (Luc, Lucian, Lux) carries associations whether or not the character is literally associated with light.
- **Irony**: A gentle name for a brutal character, or a grand name for a humble one, creates tension between name and nature.

## The Name Brief

When requesting or evaluating names, use a structured brief:

1. Entity type: Character / Faction / Place / Title / Ability / Artifact / Arc
2. Genre and tone: Fantasy / Sci-fi / Historical / Contemporary / Comedic / Dark
3. Cultural or linguistic roots: Specify real-world language families if applicable.
4. Symbolic intent: What the name should suggest (strength, mystery, decay, transcendence).
5. Length and pronounceability constraints: Syllable count, ease of reading.
6. Existing names in system: Names already in use to avoid collision.

## Consistency Within Naming Systems

A world's names should feel like they come from the same linguistic family:

- Same phonological rules (no "Khazad-dûm" next to "Bob" without in-world explanation).
- Same naming conventions for similar entity types.
- Translated names for common concepts ("Silverwood" vs "Argenwood" — pick one convention and stay with it).
- Faction prefixes and suffixes consistent within the faction.

## Real-World Source Inspiration

Draw from history, mythology, and language without direct copying:

- **Mythology**: Use archetypes and domains rather than exact divine names. Borrow motifs, not proper nouns.
- **History**: Adapt titles and naming conventions from the relevant period. A fantasy empire can use Roman naming conventions without calling anyone Julius.
- **Etymology**: Research the root language of a meaningful word and derive names from related vocabulary.
- **Geography**: Adapt real place names by shifting syllables or combining elements from different regions.

## Avoiding Accidental Associations

Before finalising any name, test it:

- Search for the name online to check for existing notable figures, brands, or controversial associations.
- Read the name aloud for unintended phonetic similarity to offensive terms in English and likely audience languages.
- Consider nicknames and abbreviations a reader might naturally derive.
- Check initials of multi-word names for unintended acronyms.

# Emotional Interiority

## The Reaction Continuum

When an event occurs in limited POV, the character's response follows a predictable sequence. Cutting steps in this sequence produces a flat or report-style narration.

1. **Instantaneous perception** — The sensory datum. A sound, a sight, a smell. Registered before the mind interprets it.
2. **Sensory amplification** — The body reacts before the mind catches up. Pupils dilate. Skin flushes. Stomach clenches.
3. **Emotional spike** — The raw feeling. Fear. Anger. Joy. Shame. Unmediated.
4. **Cognitive labeling** — The mind names the feeling. "I am afraid." This step is optional and often skipped in strong emotion.
5. **Physical action** — The body moves. Flinch. Reach. Step back. Lunge.
6. **Deliberate response** — The character chooses how to react. This is where performance (masking, deflection) begins.

Writing tip: To slow a moment, extend the sequence. To accelerate, compress or skip steps.

## Filtering vs Direct Experience

Filtering removes the reader from the character's immediate experience.

- **Weak (filtered)**: "She felt a cold dread wash over her as she realized the door was locked."
- **Strong (direct)**: "The door was locked. Cold dread washed over her."
- Common filter verbs to eliminate: *felt, realized, noticed, saw, heard, knew, thought, seemed, appeared.*
- The exception: Use filters intentionally when the character is dissociating, drugged, or in shock — the filter becomes the point.

## Showing Internal States Without Tell-Words

"Tell" words announce an emotion without embodying it. Replace them with sensory evidence the reader can interpret.

| Avoid (Tell) | Use Instead (Show) |
|---|---|
| He was angry | His jaw tightened. His voice went flat. |
| She was nervous | She twisted the ring on her finger until it left a red mark. |
| He was sad | He stared at the wall for a long time. Then he stood and walked out. |
| She was happy | A laugh escaped her before she could stop it. |

The goal is not to eliminate emotional vocabulary entirely — direct naming has its place — but to ground each emotional beat in physical, sensory reality before naming it.

## Emotional Layering

Characters rarely feel a single emotion. Multiple simultaneous or conflicting emotions create depth.

- **Primary + secondary**: Fear masked as anger. Grief buried under exhaustion. Joy shadowed by guilt.
- **Sequential layering**: The character feels one thing, then another as cognition catches up. "Relief flooded her — then horror as she understood what she had done."
- **Contradictory simultaneity**: "She loved him and she wanted him gone."
- Layer emotions by stacking body cues: A character can have trembling hands (fear) and clenched teeth (anger) simultaneously, conveying a complex state without naming it.

## Body Cues for Specific Emotions

Build a personal lexicon of physical responses to avoid repetitious cues.

- **Fear**: Cold extremities, shallow rapid breath, tunnel vision, hyperacusis (sounds too loud), startle response, nausea.
- **Anger**: Heat (face, chest), jaw clenching, raised voice or deliberate quiet, pacing or dangerous stillness, reduced impulse control.
- **Grief**: Heaviness in limbs, hollow sensation in chest, tears delayed or absent, dissociation, slowed speech, forgetting.
- **Shame**: Heat in face, desire to shrink or hide, avoiding eye contact, covering gestures (hand to mouth, arms crossed), stammering.
- **Joy**: Lightness, laughter, increased gesture, widened eyes, warmth, restlessness, the urge to share.
- **Anxiety**: Fidgeting, repetitive motion, scanning, dry mouth, racing thoughts, compulsive checking.

Each character should have a signature set of body cues distinct from other characters. Two characters feeling fear should manifest it differently based on personality, history, and training.

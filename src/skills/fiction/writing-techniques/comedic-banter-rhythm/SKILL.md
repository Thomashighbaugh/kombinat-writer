# Comedic Banter Rhythm

## Banter Rhythm

Effective banter follows a cadence built on paired structures:

- **Setup and punchline**: The first speaker creates an expectation; the second subverts it. The subversion must be sharper or more surprising than the setup.
- **Straight man and funny man**: One character plays the earnest foil while the other bends reality. The straight man's sincerity amplifies the comic's absurdity.
- **Escalation**: Each exchange raises the stakes of the joke. Start with observation, move to insult, crest into absurdity, then release.
- **Callbacks**: A reference to an earlier exchange rewards attentive readers and creates continuity in long dialogue scenes.

## Comedic Escalation Patterns

| Pattern | Structure | Effect |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| **Three-beat** | Setup, setup, punchline | The third line defies the pattern of the first two |
| **Inversion** | Character contradicts their own established belief | Comedy from hypocrisy or self-awareness |
| **Piling on** | Multiple characters add increasingly absurd details | Group dynamics and one-upmanship |
| **Understatement** | Extreme situation met with mild reaction | Contrast between event and response |

## Humour Types by Character

Match comedic style to character psychology:

- **Deadpan**: Delivered without affect. The humour is in the gap between the horrific situation and the character's flat delivery.
- **Self-deprecating**: Character lowers their own status. Use for likeable but insecure characters; overuse weakens authority.
- **Sarcastic**: Irony as weapon. Effective for adversarial banter but signals genuine hostility if unrelieved by warmth.
- **Absurd**: Reality-bending logic. Best for characters whose worldview does not match the situation, or for moments of psychological break.

## Argument-Driven Exposition

Conflict can deliver information more naturally than narration:

- One character reveals a fact by accusing another of it.
- A plan is disclosed through one character rejecting it as foolish.
- History emerges through one character holding a grudge about past events.
- The reader learns the rules of a system through one character exploiting them and another objecting.

Each piece of information must serve both the argument and the reader's understanding. If it only serves the reader, it feels planted.

## Tonal Boundaries

Comedy can sabotage tension if placed without regard for the emotional frame:

- **Do not use comedy**: During genuine loss, at the moment of a villain's triumph, immediately after a character death, or when the POV character is in acute danger.
- **Use comedy to relieve tension**: After a crisis resolves, as a transition between intense scenes, or to characterise a figure who refuses to acknowledge danger.
- **Beware the quip cascade**: When every character starts trading one-liners under pressure, the scene loses all sense of threat. Let one character stay serious to anchor the tension.

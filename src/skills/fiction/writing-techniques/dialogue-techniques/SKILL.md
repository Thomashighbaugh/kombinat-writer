# Dialogue Techniques

## Subtext — Saying One Thing, Meaning Another

Subtext is the engine of compelling dialogue. Characters rarely state their true intent directly. Every line should imply more than it states.

- **Avoid on-the-nose dialogue**: If a character is angry, do not have them say "I am angry." Have them say something cutting about an unrelated topic, or deflect with false cheer.
- **Layered intention**: Each line should serve at least two purposes — advancing surface plot while revealing or concealing emotional truth.
- **Misdirection as character**: A character who lies habitually reveals truth through what they avoid saying.

## Speech as Action

Dialogue is not information exchange; it is a character performing an action with words. Every utterance is an attempt to change the situation.

- Replace speech tags with action beats where possible. "She said" is invisible; "He slammed the mug down" carries weight.
- Ask: *What is this character trying to do to the other person with these words?* Threaten? Placate? Manipulate? Court?
- Conflict drives dialogue. If both characters agree for more than three exchanges, cut or escalate.

## Character Voice Differentiation

Each character must be distinguishable on the page without a tag.

- **Syntax fingerprints**: One character uses short, clipped sentences; another, long, winding clauses with qualifiers.
- **Vocabulary register**: A professor uses precise Latinate terms; a dockworker uses concrete Anglo-Saxon words.
- **Tics and rhythms**: Recurring filler words ("well," "look," "honestly"), sentence-initial conjunctions, tendency to ask questions vs make statements.
- Run a blind test: Cover the dialogue tags and see if you can identify who is speaking.

## Silence and Hesitation

What is not said is as important as what is.

- **Pauses as punctuation**: An ellipsis or a beat can indicate a character withholding, processing, or lying.
- **Topic shifts as evasion**: When a character changes the subject, the avoided topic is the important one.
- **Silence as power**: The character who is comfortable with silence controls the conversation.

## Dialect and Register Choices

Dialect marks belonging; register marks class, education, and context.

- **Hint, don't transcribe**: A few well-placed nonstandard spellings ("goin'" vs "going") signal dialect without exhausting the reader.
- **Code-switching**: A character who speaks differently at work, at home, and among friends reveals multiple selves.
- **Register collision**: When a formal character uses slang, or a casual character is forced into formality, the shift carries weight.

## Exposition Through Dialogue

Information must be revealed without characters telling each other what they already know ("as you know, Bob").

- **The novice and the expert**: One character explains to another who genuinely needs the information.
- **Discovery in conversation**: Characters learn something together, or one realizes the implication of known facts mid-speech.
- **Argument as reveal**: When characters disagree, they surface assumptions and backstory organically.

## Group Conversation Dynamics

Three or more speakers require choreography.

- **Triangulation**: A speaks to B about C, using C as a proxy for the real message.
- **Subgroups**: Side conversations within a larger group create texture and can signal alliances.
- **Overlap and interruption**: Who interrupts whom reveals power hierarchy. A character who is consistently talked over is being silenced.
- **The silent observer**: The character who says nothing in a group scene often carries the most dramatic weight.

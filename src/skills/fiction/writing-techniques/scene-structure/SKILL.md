# Scene Structure

## Dwight Swain's Scene / Sequel Model

Every effective narrative unit alternates between two modes: **Scene** (external action) and **Sequel** (internal processing).

### Scene: Goal → Conflict → Disaster

- **Goal**: The POV character enters the scene wanting something specific and immediate. The goal must be concrete, measurable, and opposed. A weak goal produces a weak scene.
- **Conflict**: Obstacles between the character and the goal. Conflict escalates: each attempt to overcome an obstacle should raise the stakes or introduce a new complication.
- **Disaster**: The scene ends in failure. The character does not get what they wanted — or gets it at an unacceptable cost. The disaster can be a reversal (expectation subverted), a setback (goal denied), or an revelation (new information that changes everything).

### Sequel: Reaction → Dilemma → Decision

- **Reaction**: The character's visceral, emotional response to the disaster. This is the pause before action — shock, grief, fear, anger. Emotional interiority is critical here.
- **Dilemma**: The character faces a forced choice with no good options. The dilemma must be meaningful: each option carries genuine cost and reveals character through what is chosen.
- **Decision**: The character makes a choice that generates the goal for the next scene. The decision must be active, not passive — the character must act, not merely react.

## Scene Types

- **Action Scene**: External conflict drives the scene. High stakes, ticking clock, physical or verbal confrontation. Follows Scene pattern tightly.
- **Reaction Scene**: Internal processing dominates. The character absorbs events, reels, recalibrates. Follows Sequel pattern.
- **Setup Scene**: Establishes context, stakes, or character state before a major sequence. Danger: can be static. Ensure setup contains micro-conflict.
- **Payoff Scene**: Delivers on a promise made earlier (a confrontation, a revelation, a resolution). The emotional weight depends on how well the setup was laid.

## Scene Goals

- Each scene must answer: *What does the POV character want right now, and why can't they have it?*
- Goals should escalate across the story. Early scenes have external, tactical goals (find the key). Later scenes have internal, strategic goals (decide who to trust).
- Every scene goal must tie to the story's central conflict. A scene that does not advance the core tension may be elegant but is extraneous.

## Conflict Escalation

- **Three-beat conflict**: First attempt fails (minor setback). Second attempt costs something (sacrifice). Third attempt produces disaster (game over for this round).
- Conflict should not repeat. Each attempt should attack the obstacle from a different angle or with greater stakes.
- If the character can walk away from the conflict, the stakes are too low.

## Disaster Types

- **Reversal**: The situation flips. The ally reveals betrayal. The safe path becomes deathtrap.
- **Setback**: The character loses ground. Progress made earlier is undone. The goal moves further away.
- **Revelation**: The character gains new information that changes the meaning of everything that came before. Often more devastating than physical setbacks.

## Scene Pacing

- Scenes open late and close early. Enter as close to the conflict as possible. Exit as soon as the disaster lands.
- Vary scene length. A sequence of short scenes accelerates pace; a long scene signals importance and allows immersion.
- Summary bridges compress time between scenes. Use summary for low-tension transitions; use scene for high-tension moments.

## Scene Transitions

- **Hard cut**: End one scene, begin the next with no connective tissue. Effective for contrast or acceleration.
- **Temporal bridge**: "Three hours later..." Signals passage of unimportant time.
- **Spatial shift**: Move to a different location to indicate a new scene. Works best when the location carries thematic weight.
- **Linked by decision**: The Sequel's Decision generates the next scene's Goal. This is the gold standard — every scene grows organically from the last.

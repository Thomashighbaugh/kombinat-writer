# Character Depth

## The Wound/Ghost Model

Every compelling character carries a psychic wound — a past event that shaped their core beliefs about themselves and the world.

- **The Ghost**: A specific, named event (a betrayal, a death, a failure) that the character may never speak of but that informs every decision.
- **The Lie**: The false belief the character adopted to survive the wound. ("I can trust no one." "I am not enough." "Love is dangerous.")
- **The Truth**: What the character must learn by story's end. The arc is the journey from Lie to Truth.
- Apply the wound backward: From a character's most irrational behavior, deduce the wound that produced it.

## Internal Contradictions

Flat characters are consistent. Real people are contradictory.

- **Virtue and flaw from the same trait**: Stubbornness is also perseverance. Caution is also cowardice. Generosity is also self-neglect.
- **Situational inconsistency**: A brave soldier afraid of intimacy. A ruthless executive who rescues stray animals.
- **The public self vs the private self**: How the character performs for others vs who they are alone.
- Use contradiction to generate plot: The character's conflicting desires (safety vs freedom, duty vs love) create the story's central tension.

## Defense Mechanisms

Characters behave as real people do — they protect themselves from psychic pain through unconscious strategies.

- **Rationalization**: Inventing logical excuses for emotional decisions.
- **Projection**: Accusing others of the flaws the character cannot accept in themselves.
- **Reaction formation**: Expressing the opposite of the true feeling (exaggerated disdain masking attraction).
- **Sublimation**: Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable behavior (rage into discipline, grief into work).
- Each defense mechanism should fail under story pressure, forcing the character to confront what they have avoided.

## Vulnerability Triggers

Know what makes the character flinch, deflect, or attack.

- **Core triggers**: Specific words, situations, or dynamics that activate the wound. A character abandoned as a child may panic at a partner's silence.
- **The mask slip**: The moment the composed exterior cracks — a flash of genuine reaction before the character regains control.
- **Who sees behind the mask**: The one character (ally or enemy) who can read the vulnerability and exploit or protect it.

## The Iceberg Principle of Revealed vs Hidden Character

Ninety percent of the character's history, psychology, and inner life remains off the page.

- The writer must know everything about the character — childhood, education, first love, deepest shame — but only ten percent appears in text.
- The hidden mass provides consistency. A character's reactions feel authentic because the writer understands their source, even if the reader never learns it.
- Use inference: Let the reader deduce the hidden from the revealed. A character who cannot enter elevators implies a past trauma without flashback.

## Character Voice as Psychology Expression

A character's voice is not accent or slang — it is the sound of their psychology.

- **Syntax as worldview**: A character who sees the world as threatening uses short, defensive constructions. A character who sees opportunity uses open, speculative language.
- **Metaphor signature**: One character thinks in mechanical metaphors (gears, levers, engines); another in organic ones (growth, decay, harvest). The choice reveals inner landscape.
- **Obsessive return**: The words, images, or themes a character returns to under stress reveal their preoccupations.
- Voice should shift under pressure: a controlled character's sentences fragment when the defense fails; a chaotic one's speech clarifies in crisis.

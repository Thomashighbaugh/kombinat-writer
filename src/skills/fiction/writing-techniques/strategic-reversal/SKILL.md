# Strategic Reversal

## Contest Setup

Define the explicit framework before the action begins:

- **Rules**: What is permitted, forbidden, and left ambiguous. The gaps in rules are as important as the rules themselves.
- **Win conditions**: What each participant believes they need to achieve. Surface goals often mask real objectives.
- **Participant assumptions**: What each side takes for granted about the contest, their opponent, or the environment. Every assumption is a future lever.
- **Stakes and constraints**: Time pressure, resource limits, audience expectations, and reputational cost.

## Hidden Levers

Reversals require asymmetry that the reader can trace after the reveal:

- **Information asymmetry**: One party knows something the other does not — a weakness, a vulnerability, a third party's intention.
- **Rule exploits**: A character wins by satisfying the letter but violating the spirit of the rules. The exploit must be foreshadowed.
- **Hidden resources**: Allies, tools, knowledge, or authority revealed at the critical moment. Each must be planted earlier in the narrative.
- **Misdirection**: A visible effort that exists to draw attention away from the real play.

## Reversal Types

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Power reversal** | The weaker party wins through strategy | Outcast defeats champion via environmental trap |
| **Information reversal** | What was "known" proves false | The macguffin was a decoy; real prize was elsewhere |
| **Status reversal** | Low-status character revealed as high | The fool is the king in disguise |
| **Allegiance reversal** | Apparent ally or enemy switches sides | The betrayer was a double agent all along |

## Dramatic Irony in Reversals

The reader should sense the trap before the victim does. Plant clues at three levels:

1. **Surface level**: What the POV character notices and interprets.
2. **Subtext level**: What the reader can infer from behaviour, setting details, or offhand comments.
3. **Structural level**: The story's own patterns — a character who has won every contest so far is due for a loss.

## Fair-Play Principles

A satisfying reversal is surprising yet inevitable on rereading:

- **Plant clues early**: The hidden resource or rule exploit must exist in the text before the reveal, not invented at the moment of need.
- **Foreshadow without announcing**: A character glancing at a loose floorboard in Act I becomes the hiding place in Act III.
- **Respect established abilities**: A character cannot suddenly possess a skill that the narrative has never suggested they have.
- **Test the reversal against the rules**: Would the contest's adjudicator accept this outcome? If not, the reversal should fail or the adjudicator should be corrupt.
